### Scientific Explanation

On a deeper level, gravity can be explained by Newton’s law of universal gravitation: Every point mass attracts every other point mass in the universe with a force that is directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. Mathematically, this can be represented as:

\[ F = = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2} \]

- \(F\) is the gravitational force between two objects.
- \(G\) (gravitational constant) is a proportionality constant that depends on the units chosen for mass and distance.
- \(m_1\) and \(m_2\) are the masses of the two objects.
- \(r\) is the distance between their centers.

### General Relativity

Newton's law provides a good approximation at everyday scales, but when we consider large-scale phenomena like gravity near massive objects (like stars or black holes), we need to use Einstein’s theory of general relativity. According to this view, gravity is not just an attractive force, but rather it results from the curvature of space and time caused by mass and energy.

Dosa is a savory crepe-like dish from South India that is popular in several countries including Sri Lanka and Malaysia. Here’s a simple recipe to make dosas:

### Ingredients:
- 1 cup whole wheat flour (besan or urad dal for non-vegetarian dosa)
- Water as needed (about 2 cups total, depending on the consistency you prefer)
- Salt to taste
- Oil for cooking (about 3 tablespoons)

### Instructions:

#### Making the Dosa Batter:
1. In a large bowl, mix the whole wheat flour with salt.
2. Slowly add water while continuously stirring until you get a thick and smooth batter. The batter should not be too thin or too thick; it should have a consistency similar to that of yogurt.

#### Cooking the Dosa:
3. Heat about 1 tablespoon oil in a large, heavy-bottomed pan (like an idli cooker) over medium heat.
4. Once the oil is heated and starts shimmering, pour enough batter to cover the base of the pan (about 2-3 tablespoons per batch depending on how thick or thin you like your dosas). Use a ladle or small bowl to measure out the batter for each dosa.
5. When the edges start curling up from the sides, use a spatula to flip it and cook on the other side until golden brown spots appear (about 2-3 minutes per side).
6. Remove the dosa from the pan and keep warm while cooking the remaining batter.

### Serving:
Serve the dosas hot with sambar (vegetable stew), chutney, or coconut chutney for dipping. You can also add vegetables like onions, tomatoes, potatoes, and green peas as fillings before rolling them up.

#### Tips:
- To get a non-greasy dosa, you can use less oil in the pan.
- You can experiment with different types of vegetables to make flavored dosas or even vegan dosas by using plant-based milk instead of buttermilk for fermentation.

Enjoy your homemade dosas!

2. **Spacetime Curvature**: The presence of a massive object (like Earth or the Sun) causes its surrounding spacetime to curve more than it would in the absence of the object's mass/energy. Objects are "pulled" towards this region due to the curvature, even if they start off moving away from it.

3. **Gravity as Motion**: In a sense, gravity is not a force acting between two bodies; instead, it’s seen as a consequence of objects trying to follow straight paths (geodesics) in curved spacetime. 4. **Equivalence Principle**: This principle states that the effects of gravity are indistinguishable from acceleration in an otherwise inertial frame. 5. **General Relativity and the Equations**: Einstein formulated these ideas into his equations of general relativity. These equations describe how mass/energy can cause spacetime to curve, which in turn affects how objects move.

6. **Predictive Power**: General relativity makes accurate predictions about the motion of planets (as seen in orbital mechanics) and even predicts phenomena like black holes and gravitational waves, which have been confirmed by observations.

It's important to note that while general relativity is a very good approximation for gravity at macroscopic scales, quantum mechanics provides more accurate descriptions at subatomic levels. However, these two theories do not yet coexist seamlessly, leading to ongoing research into quantum gravity theories like string theory and loop quantum gravity.

Dosa is a savory Indian/Udupi pancake made from fermented rice and black lentils (urad dal). Here's a basic recipe to make dosa:

### Ingredients:
- 1 cup finely ground urad dal (black lentil)
- 2 cups water
- 1/2 teaspoon salt
- 1 tablespoon oil or ghee, for frying

### Preparation Steps:

#### For the Batter:
1. **Combine Dry Ingredients**: In a large bowl, mix together the urad dal and salt.
2. **Add Water Gradually**: Slowly add the water to the dry ingredients while stirring continuously with your hand or a spoon until you get a smooth batter without any lumps. The batter should be thick enough to hold its shape but not too runny.

#### For Cooking:
1. **Prepare the Dosa Batter**: After mixing, cover the bowl and let it ferment in a warm place for about 4-6 hours (overnight is ideal). This step allows the urad dal to release some of their starches and creates a slightly sour flavor.
2. **Cooking the Dosas**:
   - Heat oil or ghee in a large non-stick skillet over medium heat.
   - Pour ladlefuls of dosa batter onto the hot skillet, spreading it out with your spatula to form thin circles.
   - Allow one side to cook until bubbles appear on top and turn golden brown. This takes about 30-45 seconds.
   - Flip the dosa gently and let the other side also get golden brown for another 15-20 seconds.
   - Slide onto a plate, repeat with remaining batter.
   
### Serving:
Serve hot along with various condiments such as sambar (vegetable stew), coconut chutney, or raita. 

Remember that dosa is more of an art form than just a recipe. Practice makes perfect when it comes to making dosas look and taste their best!
